Understanding the Speed of Light Scale

The speed of light in vacuum, denoted c, is a universal constant exactly defined as 299,792,458 meters per second, fundamental to modern physics, electromagnetism, and the theory of relativity.

History of the Speed of Light Scale

First accurately measured by Ole Rømer in 1676 using Jupiter's moons, the speed of light was refined through experiments by Fizeau, Foucault, and Michelson. It was fixed in 1983 by redefining the meter based on c, ensuring ultimate precision in scientific measurements.

Understanding the Miles per Hour Scale

Miles per hour (mph) is an imperial unit of speed, indicating the distance in miles traveled in one hour, widely used in the United States and United Kingdom for road transport, aviation, and weather reporting.

History of the Miles per Hour Scale

Derived from the Roman mile, mph was formalized in the British Imperial system in 1824 and standardized internationally in 1959. It remains key in non-metric countries for speed limits, vehicle performance, and meteorological data.

The Conversion Formula Explained

The formula to convert a fraction of the speed of light to miles per hour is:

Where mph is miles per hour and c is the fraction of the speed of light. This derives from the speed of light being exactly 670,616,629.384 mph.

FAQ

Why convert between c and mph? Conversions are vital in physics education, relativity simulations, and high-energy particle research where speeds are expressed as fractions of c but compared to familiar units.

Is there a simple estimation method? Multiply the fraction of c by 6.7 × 10^8 for a quick approximate in mph, then refine for accuracy.
