Understanding the Gram Scale

The gram is a metric unit of mass, equal to one thousandth of a kilogram, widely used in scientific measurements, cooking recipes, and pharmaceutical dosing for its precision and ease of use in decimal calculations.

History of the Gram Scale

Introduced during the French Revolution in 1795 as part of the metric system, the gram was originally defined as the mass of one cubic centimeter of water at 4Â°C, later refined with the kilogram prototype and now tied to fundamental constants.

Understanding the Ounce Scale

The ounce is an imperial unit of mass, standardized as 28.349523125 grams in the avoirdupois system, commonly used in the United States for food portions, postal weights, and precious metals trading.

History of the Ounce Scale

Originating from the Roman uncia (one-twelfth of a libra), the ounce evolved through medieval trade systems, standardized in 1824 in Britain and 1959 internationally, distinguishing between troy ounces for metals and avoirdupois for general use.
