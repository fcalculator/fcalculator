Understanding the Chi-Square Test

The Chi-Square test evaluates whether observed categorical data deviates significantly from expected values. It is commonly applied in hypothesis testing for independence between variables or goodness-of-fit to a distribution.

Key Formula

The Chi-Square statistic is calculated as:

\[ \chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O_i - E_i)^2}{E_i} \]

Where \(O_i\) is the observed frequency and \(E_i\) is the expected frequency for each cell.

Degrees of freedom (df) for a contingency table: \(df = (r-1)(c-1)\), with r rows and c columns.

Assumptions and Limitations

Ensure expected frequencies are at least 5 in 80% of cells. For small samples, consider alternatives like Fisher's exact test.

FAQ

What is the difference between goodness-of-fit and independence tests? Goodness-of-fit compares one variable to a distribution, while independence checks relationships between two variables.

When should I use Yates' correction? Apply it for 2x2 tables with small expected frequencies to improve accuracy.

How does sample size affect the test? Larger samples improve reliability, but very large samples may detect trivial differences.
