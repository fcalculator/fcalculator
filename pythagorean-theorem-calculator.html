Detailed Explanation of the Pythagorean Theorem

In a right-angled triangle, the theorem quantifies the relationship as a² + b² = c², where a and b are the legs adjacent to the right angle, and c is the hypotenuse opposite it. This equation derives from area conservation principles in geometric rearrangements.

For instance, with a = 5 and b = 12, c = √(25 + 144) = √169 = 13. Such calculations underpin distance metrics in Cartesian planes and form the basis for trigonometric identities like sine and cosine definitions.

The theorem extends to the law of cosines for non-right triangles: c² = a² + b² - 2ab cos(θ), reducing to the Pythagorean form when θ = 90° and cos(90°) = 0.

Mathematical Derivation of the Pythagorean Theorem

The Pythagorean theorem states that in a right-angled triangle with legs a and b, and hypotenuse c, the equation a² + b² = c² holds true. To arrive at this solution mathematically, consider the following step-by-step process:

  1. Define the right triangle: One angle is exactly 90 degrees, with sides a and b forming the right angle, and c opposite it.
  2. Construct squares on each side: The area of the square on c equals the sum of areas on a and b, leading to the equation through area equivalence.
  3. Algebraic derivation: Start from geometric rearrangement. For example, arrange four right triangles around a square of side c, forming a larger square of side (a + b). The area equality gives (a + b)² = c² + 4*(ab/2), simplifying to a² + 2ab + b² = c² + 2ab, thus a² + b² = c².
  4. To solve for a missing side: If a and b are known, c = √(a² + b²). For a given c and b, a = √(c² - b²), ensuring c > b to avoid imaginary results.
  5. Example solution: Given a=3, b=4. Compute c = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5. Verification: 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25 = 5².

This derivation is fundamental in vector algebra, where the magnitude of the resultant vector in orthogonal directions follows the same principle. For non-Euclidean geometries, modifications apply, but in standard plane geometry, this holds universally.

Algebraic and Geometric Proofs

Among numerous demonstrations, algebraic proofs utilize area equivalences. Consider rearranging four identical right triangles around a square of side c, forming a larger square of side a + b with area (a + b)² = c² + 2ab, simplifying to a² + b² = c².

An alternative arrangement encloses a square of side b - a, with area (b - a)² + 2ab = a² + b², equating to c² for the outer formation.
