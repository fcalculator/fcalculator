The Pythagorean theorem represents a cornerstone of Euclidean geometry, establishing the relationship between the sides of a right-angled triangle. This principle asserts that the square of the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides, enabling precise calculations in fields ranging from architecture to physics. Our calculator facilitates input of any two sides to compute the third, supported by interactive visualizations and detailed derivations for educational and professional use.

Understanding this theorem enhances problem-solving in trigonometry and coordinate geometry, where right triangles form the basis for distance formulas and vector analyses. Historical records attribute the theorem to Pythagoras of Samos, though evidence suggests earlier knowledge in Babylonian mathematics. Modern applications include GPS technology, engineering designs, and computer graphics rendering.

Detailed Explanation of the Pythagorean Theorem

In a right-angled triangle, the theorem quantifies the relationship as a² + b² = c², where a and b are the legs adjacent to the right angle, and c is the hypotenuse opposite it. This equation derives from area conservation principles in geometric rearrangements.

For instance, with a = 5 and b = 12, c = √(25 + 144) = √169 = 13. Such calculations underpin distance metrics in Cartesian planes and form the basis for trigonometric identities like sine and cosine definitions.

The theorem extends to the law of cosines for non-right triangles: c² = a² + b² - 2ab cos(θ), reducing to the Pythagorean form when θ = 90° and cos(90°) = 0.

Algebraic and Geometric Proofs

Among numerous demonstrations, algebraic proofs utilize area equivalences. Consider rearranging four identical right triangles around a square of side c, forming a larger square of side a + b with area (a + b)² = c² + 2ab, simplifying to a² + b² = c².

An alternative arrangement encloses a square of side b - a, with area (b - a)² + 2ab = a² + b², equating to c² for the outer formation.
