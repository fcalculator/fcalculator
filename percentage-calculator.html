Mathematical Foundations of Percentages

The core formula is \( P = \frac{\part}{\whole} \times 100 \). For instance, 50 out of 200 is \( \frac{50}{200} \times 100 = 25\% \).

\[ P = \frac{V_1}{V_2} \times 100 \]

Change and Increase/Decrease Formulas

Change: \( \Delta P = \frac{V_{new} - V_{old}}{V_{old}} \times 100 \). Increase focuses on positive values.

\[ \Delta P = \frac{V_{new} - V_{old}}{V_{old}} \times 100 \]

Originating from Roman taxation, percentages standardize comparisons. They apply in finance for margins and in science for errors.

Applications in Various Fields

In business, markups determine pricing; in statistics, errors quantify accuracy. A 20% discount reduces demand impacts.

Discounts and Markups

Discount: Final = Original - (Discount% × Original). Markup: Selling = Cost + (Markup% × Cost).

Profit Margins and Errors

Margin: (Profit / Revenue) × 100. Error: |Observed - True| / True × 100.

FAQ

How to compute percentage increase? Subtract old from new, divide by old, multiply by 100.

What is profit margin? Profit as percentage of selling price.

How does percentage error work? Measures deviation from true value as percentage.

Can decreases be negative? Yes, indicating reduction.

What are common applications? Discounts in retail, margins in finance, errors in measurements.
