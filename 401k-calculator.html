How the 401(k) Calculator Works

This simulator forecasts 401(k) accumulation by iterating yearly cycles: adjusting salary for increases, calculating employee contributions as a percentage, applying employer matches up to limits, adding investment earnings based on the assumed rate, and updating the balance. Inputs include current age, retirement age, initial balance, salary, contribution rates, match details, salary growth, and return rate. Outputs detail projected balance, total inputs, and breakdowns via tables and charts. Assumptions encompass annual compounding, no fees or taxes modeled, and consistent parameters for baseline planning.

Mathematical Formulas and Derivations

Yearly growth is computed sequentially:

\[ \text{New Salary} = \text{Previous Salary} \times \left(1 + \frac{\text{Salary Increase \%}}{100}\right) \] \[ \text{Your Contribution} = \text{Salary} \times \frac{\text{Contribution \%}}{100} \] \[ \text{Employer Match} = \min\left(\text{Your Contribution} \times \frac{\text{Match \%}}{100}, \text{Salary} \times \frac{\text{Match Limit \%}}{100}\right) \] \[ \text{Total Addition} = \text{Your Contribution} + \text{Employer Match} \] \[ \text{Earnings} = (\text{Balance} + \text{Total Addition}) \times \frac{\text{Return \%}}{100} \] \[ \text{New Balance} = \text{Previous Balance} + \text{Total Addition} + \text{Earnings} \]

For approximate total without adjustments, use future value of annuity: \[ FV = P \times \frac{(1 + r)^n - 1}{r} + I \times (1 + r)^n \] where \( P \) is average annual addition, \( r \) return rate, \( n \) years, \( I \) initial balance.

Historical 401(k) Contribution Limits and Returns

Introduced in 1978, 401(k) contribution limits have steadily risen to accommodate inflation and policy changes. In 1989, the employee elective limit was $7,627, with a total limit of $30,000. By 2010, it reached $16,500 for employees, $49,000 total. For 2025, the employee limit is $23,500, total employee and employer $70,000, with catch-up contributions of $7,500 for ages 50–59 and 64+, and $11,250 for ages 60–63, pushing totals to $77,500 or $81,250. Average returns vary with market conditions: long-term (post-2000) around 5-8% after inflation, 4.6% annually from 2006-2012 for life-cycle funds, 9.7% from 2019-2024, and a strong 17.5% in 2024 according to Investopedia and Vanguard data.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a 401(k) plan? Employer-sponsored savings with pre-tax contributions and tax-deferred growth.

How does matching work? Employers add up to a percentage of your input, limited by salary portion.

What return rate to use? 5-8% historical average after inflation; adjust for risk.

Contribution limits? $23,500 employee in 2025, plus catch-up $7,500 for 50–59/64+, $11,250 for 60–63.

Withdrawal rules? Penalties before 59½; RMDs at 73.
