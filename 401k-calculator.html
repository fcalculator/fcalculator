How the 401(k) Calculator Works

A 401(k) plan is a tax-advantaged retirement savings account offered by employers in the United States. Contributions are deducted from your salary pre-tax, reducing your taxable income, and grow tax-deferred until withdrawal in retirement. Many employers offer matching contributions, effectively providing additional funds to boost your savings.

This calculator simulates the growth of your 401(k) year by year. It starts with your current balance and adds annual contributions (your percentage of salary plus employer match, capped by the match limit). Salary increases annually by the specified rate, and the balance earns compound interest at the expected return rate.

The core formula for future value with annual contributions and compounding is:

\[ FV = PV \times (1 + r)^n + PMT \times \frac{(1 + r)^n - 1}{r} \]

Where:

  • \( FV \): Future value (projected balance)
  • \( PV \): Present value (current balance)
  • \( r \): Annual return rate
  • \( n \): Number of years until retirement
  • \( PMT \): Annual payment (total contributions)

However, since contributions adjust with salary growth and employer match, the simulation runs iteratively for accuracy.

Understanding 401(k) Contributions and Matches

Your contributions are a percentage of your salary, up to IRS limits (e.g., $23,500 in 2025 for those under 50). Employer matches amplify this; for example, a 50% match up to 6% of salary means if you contribute 6%, your employer adds 3%, totaling 9% of salary saved annually.

Contribution Level Employer Match Example (50% up to 6%) Total Savings Rate
4% 2% 6%
6% 3% 9%
10% 3% 13%

Maximizing the match is crucial, as it represents "free money" toward your retirement.

Impact of Investment Returns and Fees

Expected returns typically range from 5-8% for balanced portfolios, but vary with market conditions. Higher returns come with greater risk. Fees can erode growth; aim for low-cost index funds to minimize them.

For illustration, a 7% return over 30 years can double your money roughly every 10 years due to compounding.
